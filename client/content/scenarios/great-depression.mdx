### The Crisis: What Happened and Why
The Great Depression was a "perfect storm" of speculative excess, weak banking infrastructure, and disastrous monetary policy. Following a decade of prosperity in the "Roaring Twenties," the market became dangerously overextended. When the bubble burst on Black Tuesday, it triggered a massive deleveraging event. Unlike modern crises, the lack of federal deposit insurance (FDIC) meant that bank failures resulted in the total disappearance of personal savings, leading to a decade-long economic winter.

### Winners and Losers

<WinnerBox>
- **Electric Boat:** Managed to secure early naval contracts that provided stable revenue throughout the downturn.
- **Container Corp of America:** Revolutionized industrial shipping, finding efficiency gains when every penny counted.
- **Truax-Traer Coal:** Benefited from the continued necessity of coal for heating and basic power generation.
- **Gold Miners:** As the world crashed, the fixed price of gold (under the Gold Standard) made mining exceptionally profitable relative to falling costs.
</WinnerBox>

<LoserBox>
- **Railroads:** Faced total devastation as industrial production and travel halted almost completely.
- **American Superpower:** Speculative utility holding companies that were highly leveraged collapsed as credit dried up.
- **Retail Banks:** Without the FDIC, thousands of banks went bankrupt, wiping out the wealth of the middle class.
- **Automakers:** Luxury car brands virtually disappeared as discretionary spending vanished.
</LoserBox>

### The Recovery: The Longest Road

* **The Catalyst:** The "New Deal" social programs and the abandonment of the Gold Standard provided initial relief, but it was the massive industrial mobilization of **World War II** that finally ended the Depression.
